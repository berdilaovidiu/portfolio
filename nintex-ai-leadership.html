When generative AI exploded in late 2023, Nintex, like many enterprise software companies, had a problem: engineers were racing to ship AI features in technical POCs that would become user experiences, but nobody understood how AI fundamentally changes interaction design. There was no AI design expertise, no shared language for evaluating AI patterns, and no process for ensuring genAI features would actually deliver value to users.

I saw an opportunity others missed. While most designers were waiting for AI to stabilize, I went back to school, literally taking online courses in generative AI fundamentals, and started building what would become Nintex's AI design practice. This wasn't about creating a few AI features. It was about establishing organizational AI literacy across product, design, and engineering so that everyone could make better decisions as we navigated this technological shift.

Within a year, I transformed scattered experimentation into structured practice: researched 100+ AI products, documented 45 interaction patterns, founded the AI Design Guild that grew to 7 designers, and delivered workshop series that elevated AI fluency across the entire product organization. The work wasn't just influential, it became foundational. The AI assistant guidelines and platform-level patterns I established are now being built into Nintex's design system, shaping how every product team approaches AI.

The impact was recognized formally: promoted to Staff Product Designer, appointed AI Design Lead for the organization, and given the company's most critical AI initiative (Solution Designer). But the real impact was cultural, transforming how Nintex approaches AI from "let's see what the tech can do" to "let's understand how this changes user experience."
