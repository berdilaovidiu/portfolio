Mony

Mony is a personal finance advisor who mines card transactions to display interesting stats on personal expenses. The core idea was to parse SMS alerts sent by most banks on cash withdrawals, POS payments and transfers and extract relevant data in order to show spendings by category and historical trends.

Today most banks offer similar functionalities in their apps, but in 2018 this wasn’t the case, at least not in Romania. So at that time Mony was competing with apps where users had to manually input the data and were constantly annoyed and shamed with reminders.

My Role

I played the Lead Designer and Product Manager during this time. My responsibilities ranged from needfinding, prototyping and user testing all the way up to managing the work, finding ways to build a successful business and pitching in front of angel investors and large audiences.

The Minimum Viable Product

We launched a very basic app which was able to automatically gather expenses and classify them using a hard coded mapping for some known merchants. It allowed users to filter by a specific interval, inspect each expense category and move expenses between categories, while learning the user generated mappings for a better future experience.

Improving Retention

Two months after releasing the MVP we started to look at the numbers and we weren’t satisfied with the retention rate. It was a good moment to do both qualitative and quantitative research. By that time we had acquired a few hundred users that we could reach out to and had an analytics implementation that allowed us to study every user session, besides looking at higher level flows and funnels.

After a series of interviews, surveys and lots of digging through session recordings, we came up a list of improvements:

  • Some people simply forgot about our app so we had to remind them through push notifications.
  • Users who made an effort to classify some of their unclassified expenses in the first session had better retention rates. This made us think about ways to simplify the flow make it more obvious and rewarding.
  • Others complained about the app being too boring and not showing anything new on subsequent visits. We added animations to highlight what’s new in each spending category and prompted users to review the list of new transactions.
  • Gamification techniques for encouraging wanted behaviour like: classifying a transaction, emptying the list of unclassified transactions, visiting the app each week.

I had initially resisted push notifications because the competing apps were abusing them, quickly becoming annoying and constantly nagging and shaming users for forgetting to add their daily expenses. I was wrong, push notifications are essential for obtaining good retention, even for the best apps out there.

To avoid making the same mistakes, we implemented smarter notifications. They took into account user activity, had good timing, useful content and spoke in an encouraging voice, never condemning users for their behaviour.

Premium feature

The project was treated from the beginning as a learning experience for everyone involved. Even though the product was not ready for monetization yet, we had to go through this step in order to learn how sell something. This was a first for the entire team so we had to learn how to choose prices, how to publish products in the app store, how to market ourselves and how to sell.

The simplest thing to do was to come up with a premium feature. Whereas the free version allowed viewing totals by category for a chosen period, the premium feature showed historical data, by category, as a stream of expenses. This made it possible to track progress and added value to the more committed users.

Manual Entries

The vision was to create a product that needs as little manual intervention as possible, in contrast to the competing apps who relied heavily on manual input of expenses. What’s ironic is that people still needed the feature. By talking to users we found that in order to fully commit to tracking expenses as a habit, the numbers needed to be accurate, otherwise people would simply lose motivation.

Cash withdrawals, e-wallet top-ups and transfers were impossible to classify automatically and most of the time the money was spent in more than one place. Because the information was mostly in the user’s memory, having a way to manually input a part of the expenses became a necessity.

Because we had knowledge of the amounts withdrawn and transferred, the dates and sometimes the locations, we were able to build a smarter version. That combined with app usage information, allowed us to also send smarter notifications.

  • smart triggers
  • fast input
  • helpful information

Second premium feature

Trends offered a good overview for historical data but it lacked the ability to answer more specific and insightful questions like:

  • how much have I spent this december compared to last december?
  • do people with similar age, income, living in the same city spend more or less than I do?
  • how much would the same lifestyle cost me in another country or city?
  • how much more am I spending on bills in the winter months as opposed to summer months?
  • how have our expenses changed since the arrival of the newborn?

We were quite excited about this feature, but unfortunately it never saw the light of day because the project was gutted.

Learnings

Mony was such a rich learning experience and this makes it very difficult to reduce the lessons learned to a few sentences. I’ll try.

Wearing the Product Manager hat for the first time made me a better designer. Every design decision was rooted in user needs, business needs and very tight engineering and budget constraints.

The most valuable part of the design process starts once you can measure the results. The analytics tool allowed us to study user flows, build conversion funnels and compute retention rates. By looking at data, we were able to formulate hypothesis, come up with improvements and track the results.

The Pirate metrics (Aquisition, Activation, Retention, Referrals , Revenue) is a very useful framework for prioritising product decisions. It is so simple to understand and brings a lot of light into the design process as well.

As I said, the list is long. I guess the simplest way to put it is that after this experience I felt like UX design no longer describes what I do. It slowly transformed me into a Product Designer.
