ISOPROFILER

THE NEED

When I got involved in this project, a beta version had already been released and the feedback received from the early adopters was not great, both in terms of functionality and usability. The plan was to analyze the findings and come up with an improved design to take care of the shortcomings of the first prototype.

The primary need was way for scientists analyze RNA-Seq experiments in order to find interesting isoforms, along with their involvement in various diseases and biological functions, all backed by published evidence.

PREVIOUS BETA VERSION

The prototype released as a beta version had already done the heavy lifting in terms of converting the datasets to the right format, creating the data structures, analyzing the data and finding the evindence from a large knowledge base of manually curated content.

The major issue was the presentation of the results that was done by the way of a huge filterable table with a two level hierarchy. More than that, a large part of the input data couldn't be analyzed because by that point not a lot had been published on isoforms, so it was left out from the results, creating confusion and indignation for the users.

RESEARCH

Since I was not familiar with the biological context around the new feature, I had to do a lot of research to truly understand what isoforms are, how they are measured, the limitations of the current technologies and the challenges scientists face when dealing with the new type of experiment data. This gave me a pretty good idea on the tools and processes, as well as the terminology and the visual language used in the filed.

Having done the research, I was ready to review the user interviews done on the beta release. Luckily, I had recordings of three participants who offered a lot of good feedback that allowed me to identify the main pain points and some interesting new opportunities.

The main takaways were:

  • focus needs to be on the isoform expression data instead of the little literature evidence available
  • the large volumes of data are hard to digest and a drill down approach is preferable
  • there are very specific data patterns of interest

VISUAL ISOFORM REPRESENTATION

In order to make the interesting expression data patterns easy to spot, I took a more visual approach and experimented with multiple ways of displaying quantitative data.

MOLECULES VIEW

The solution needed to work for singular experiments, as well as for multiple experiments that were being analyzed side by side. That is the reason why all the following mockups have both single and multiple datasets.

All the above ideas were discussed and ultimately abandoned for various reasons. The version that survived and made it in the final product is based on colored circles of proportional size and color. This worked best because it was the most scalable of the approaches. The design needed to scale both horizontally and vertically in order to accommodate tens of isoforms of the same molecule and up to 20 experiment conditions.

ISOFORMS VIEW

Once an interesting molecule has been identified in the molecules view, it can be explored in more detail in the isoforms view. This table offers precise information about expression measurements, various annotations and a visualization to help the user make sense of the data easier and also observe interesting trends.

The first version uses a line chart that makes it easy to notice patterns like isoform switching, a pattern highly demanded by beta testers. The other two versions use a stacked approach that has the advantage of being an additive representation and variations of the overall molecule expression can be tracked along variations of the individual isoforms.

We decided to go with the first version because it was the more recognizable visualization, made patterns easier to notice and needed less vertical space, which turned out to be very important because it competed with two potentially large tables.

THE FILTERS

Even though the molecules table design was improved to make data easier to consume, it was still hard to navigate a space of thousands of molecules, looking for patterns. Filters were also available in the beta version, but I decided to move away from the table column filters in order to be able to use criteria across multiple properties and to have the filters always visible.

CONCLUSIONS

The revised version project lasted for about three months and I was involved mostly in the design, but also some of the implementation around datasets, filters and table visualizations.

The beta version was very valuable because it offered us a way to get very important feedback. Usually, the prototype is a lot more cheaper and faster to build, but in some cases, it is very hard to get a genuine reaction without using real data, especially when you are dealing with large amounts of novel data that people don't yet know how to use.

User testing was done mostly on the artistic mockups and it involved presenting multiple scenarios to the beta testers that were already familiar with the beta version. The feedback was very positive since we were addressing a lot of the issues reported by the same people.
