Parking Plus is a service that saves time for drivers and optimizes revenue for parking operators. It removes the pain of finding a parking by showing up-to-date availability on a map and guiding drivers to the closest available spot, in the street or in a private garage. The experience is further improved by integrating mobile payments and bookings.

My role was to design and test the user experience for all user touch points. I collaborated closely with the project manager, the technical lead, and a few graphic designers, at different stages.

When I joined the project, I picked up from a working iOS prototype, a consumer-focused website and an administration web app that had basic functionality.

Competitor analysis

We started by compiling a list of ranked apps from both US and European app markets. I tested and evaluated them in terms of Nielsenâ€™s usability heuristics to see how Parking Plus fares compared to them. The study resulted in a list of features that set the service apart from the rest and another list of improvements needed to catch up to the competition.

A lot of competitors were pointing to parking lots on a map, some of them were showing street parking as well, but very few were displaying availability information and almost none of them were allowing bookings.

Our goal was to improve the parking experience by:
  • showing availability information for both street parking spaces and garages
  • allowing bookings in secured areas (this became a separate product)
  • offering flexible mobile payments
Storyboards

The ability to pay in advance for a guaranteed parking space was a big differentiator and we wanted to make sure we get it right from the beginning. This is why we focused a lot on the entire process of looking for parking, booking it and accessing the secured premises.

I sketched some storyboards in order to keep us grounded in the user context while exploring solutions. They also helped the engineering team get a better understanding of the use cases.

The mobile app

For the second version of the mobile app, we targeted the Android platform since it was the most popular mobile OS among the target users. Material design had just been published at that time and using it was a learning process for the entire team, both design and engineering.

Hand-drawn mockups

I sketched most flows and screens in order to discuss them with the team. Once we found a satisfactory solution, we sent them to the graphic designer for the final touch. We focused primarily on user flows like bookings and payments, revised some flows that were poorly designed in the previous prototype, like parking availability and suggestions, and covered missing screens, like the introductory tutorial.

Android mobile screens

These are just part of the tens of screens that were refined by the graphic designer. My role in this process was to keep in close touch and provide feedback and guidance. This happened over numerous Skype calls since the graphic designer was not on site, and neither was I most of the time.

Animations

The need for seamless screen transitions was born from more than just aesthetic reasons. The app was going to be used by drivers and enforcing the navigation structure and mental model through animation became a must.

I was new to this, so I had to get up to speed with the foundations of animation and with the tools that would allow me to put together a quick mockup.

After a little research, I decided to give Pixate a try. The tool was not difficult to learn, but being at the first release at that time, it provided me with a lot of headaches. It took me a couple of months to cover the most important screen transitions and I was happy to be done with it.

I learned a lot about animations during this time, but the most important lesson was to think about the communication between the app and the server because those waiting times could not be predicted and the transitions should incorporate them from the design phase. Luckily, I was able to adapt my original proposals just by adding progress bars and spinners and tweaking the timing a little bit.

The admin website

The backend services were not a priority during the MVP phase of the project, so the web interfaces were mostly designed by the engineers. One exception was the dashboard because it was being shown in demos and sales pitches, so it needed to look good. I took this opportunity to evolve the existing interface with tables of numbers into a more visual and interactive version.

I started by analyzing the data that was displayed to understand the entities and the relationships between them, in order to find suitable visualizations.

After sketching multiple ideas, I picked the ones that made the most sense and moved to Photoshop for the finer details. Even though the chord diagram was very attractive, we decided to dispatch it because it was not scalable enough.

My second proposal was the one that got implemented. It was similar in terms of layout with the image above, it just contained more donuts, stacked bar charts and an interactive heatmap for parking occupancy data over time.

Marketing materials

Having won exhibition stands to some major European IT shows, the Web Summit in Dublin among them, we were forced to start working on the marketing materials, even though we were not ready to sell or had a marketing team.

Writing copy and designing for print was something new for me. I wasn't thrilled at the idea because I had never seen myself as a particularly creative person, but I was happy to challenge myself and step outside my comfort zone. Turns out these too can be approached with an engineering mindset. I started with the structure of the message we wanted to convey and sketched some visual ways of doing that, by way of a brochure and a catalog.

This is the version that was printed and it was the result of our teamwork. I contributed by writing the copy, choosing the pictures, providing feedback on multiple graphic design iterations, and most importantly user testing with random people to assess how easy it was to understand the message and how effective it was.

The explainer video

This was the most fun part of the project. We needed a short video to tell the story of the product. The video was supposed to be used on the website and in sales pitches.

I proposed the script, got the green light from management and started working on it. We contracted a graphic artist to draw the characters and the application screens. It took a few iterations to make sure we had all the right postures and facial expressions. A couple of weeks later I had all I needed to assemble the video.

The most challenging part was the editing tool, but also keeping the video short. After multiple iterations and almost a month, I had something worth showing.

Final thoughts

Working on this project exceeded my expectations in many ways. I proved to myself that I was capable of doing the job, and that was something I needed after preparing a switch from software development to UX design for a few years.

The variety of design work and team interactions also helped me grow a lot. I worked with multiple visual designers and graphic artists, engineers, managers, both in person and remotely, and I delivered from drawings and sketches to wireframes, high fidelity screens, animations and copy.

This was a side project, something I did while having a full-time software development job. I worked mostly nights and weekends and met with the team once or twice a week to sync up.
